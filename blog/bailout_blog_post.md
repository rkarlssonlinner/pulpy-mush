# The Bailout Playbook: A Retrospective Guide to Who Gets Saved in a Banking Crisis, Why It Is Never You, and How They Get You To Pay

*A helpful guide to where your tax dollars went, the touching human stories they could have supported, and the true story about whose ass was saved instead*

---

There is a persistent theory, advanced after every financial crisis by the people who managed it, that the interventions undertaken were both necessary and broadly beneficial — that by saving the system, they saved everyone in it. This theory is partially true, which is what makes it so useful. What this accounting omits is the question of *how* the system was saved, *who specifically* received the money, and whether an alternative intervention of equivalent cost could have produced equivalent systemic stability with a radically different distribution of benefit. In each of the cases below, the answer to that last question is yes. In each case, the alternative was not chosen. This is not a coincidence. It is a pattern. And patterns, once identified, become predictions.

---

## PART ONE: THE 2008 FINANCIAL CRISIS
### A Retrospective Guide to Interventions That Have Already Happened

---

### AIG: $182 Billion

#### *Or: How to Pay Goldman Sachs Using a Dead Insurer as the Envelope*

AIG had written $440 billion in credit default swaps against mortgage-backed securities at premiums that made sense only if you assumed nationwide correlated housing default was impossible. When that assumption collapsed, the Federal Reserve and Treasury — led by Timothy Geithner, who would shortly become Treasury Secretary in an illustration of the revolving door so frictionless it generated its own electricity — concluded AIG's failure would take down every major financial institution on earth. This was probably true. What was not inevitable was paying AIG's counterparties at 100 cents on the dollar from public funds. Goldman Sachs received $12.9 billion. Société Générale $11.9 billion. Deutsche Bank $11.8 billion. No haircuts. No negotiation. The most sophisticated financial institutions in human history, holding contracts they had entered voluntarily, were paid in full before breakfast.

*What could have happened:* The same $182 billion directed as mortgage payment subsidies to every distressed household whose loan sat in the underlying MBS pools would have kept those securities performing. Performing securities don't trigger CDO defaults. Non-defaulting CDOs don't trigger CDS contracts. Non-triggering CDS contracts don't create AIG's liability. The bailout was the solution to a problem that direct household support would have prevented from existing. Treasury officials later described the household-first approach as insufficiently targeted and difficult to administer quickly at scale. The $12.9 billion wire to Goldman was administered quickly at scale the same afternoon.

### Fannie Mae & Freddie Mac: $187 Billion

#### *Or: How Foreclosed Homes Became Rental Empires, With Your Assistance*

Treasury injected $187 billion into the GSEs, which between them backstopped half of all American mortgages. The GSEs then proceeded to foreclose on five million homes — the largest mass displacement of American homeowners since the Great Depression — and sold the accumulated inventory in bulk to institutional investors. Blackstone purchased tens of thousands of single-family homes at distressed prices, founding the American single-family rental industry as an institutional asset class. Treasury eventually recovered its $187 billion plus interest and described this as a success. A cohort of households who had lost homes were now renting, in many cases in the same neighborhoods, from institutional buyers who had acquired those homes from the GSEs that had foreclosed on them. The circle was complete. It was not circular in their favor.

*What could have happened:* The GSEs owned the loans. Principal reduction — writing down underwater mortgage balances to current market value — would have imposed the same loss on the GSE balance sheet as foreclosure, but the household retains the home, the neighborhood retains its owner-occupant, and Blackstone does not acquire a generational asset class at distressed prices. The modification programs that did exist — HAMP, HARP — were systematically underfunded and administered by servicers with financial incentives to push borrowers toward foreclosure rather than modification, because the fee structure on delinquent loans exceeded the fee structure on performing ones. This was not a design oversight. It was a design.

### The Federal Reserve Balance Sheet: $0 (Officially) / $2 Trillion (Actually)

#### *Or: Monetary Policy as the Most Regressive Tax Nobody Calls a Tax*

Through Quantitative Easing, the Fed purchased $1.25 trillion in agency MBS and $200 billion in agency debt, placing a floor under the asset prices held by banks, insurance companies, and wealth-holding households. This is described as "market support" when the Fed does it and "price manipulation" when anyone else does it. The mechanism by which this "helped" ordinary households was the wealth effect: higher asset prices make wealthy people feel wealthier, wealthy people spend more, spending supports employment. The measured elasticity is approximately 0.2 — twenty cents of household consumption per dollar of QE-inflated wealth, accruing in proportion to existing financial wealth. The top quintile holds roughly 90% of financial assets. QE was a $2 trillion wealth transfer to the top quintile, administered through a central bank whose mandate contains no distributional clause.

*What could have happened:* Congress could have authorized equivalent household debt relief simultaneously, using the monetary accommodation to fund direct mortgage restructuring rather than waiting for wealth effects to trickle. The distributional outcome of equivalent macroeconomic intervention would have been unrecognizable. The decision not to couple fiscal household relief with monetary asset reflation was a choice, made by people whose professional formation had occurred entirely within the institutions that benefited most from the decoupling. Bernanke won the Nobel Prize in 2022 for showing how avoiding bank failures protected ordinary workers. It does not address whether the bank-protecting tools could have been redesigned to also protect the households. That question was not submitted for consideration.

## PART TWO: THE NEXT THREE BAILOUTS
### A Prospective Guide to Interventions That Haven't Happened Yet But Are Being Quietly Prepared

*Written, for clarity, in the past tense from a vantage point somewhere around 2029*

---

### Commercial Real Estate: The Extend-and-Pretend Graduation Ceremony

By 2026, the $1.7 trillion in commercial real estate loans quietly rolled over since 2022 were not recovering. Office vacancy had stabilized around 22%. Regional banks with CRE concentrations above 300% of equity began failing at a rate the FDIC described as "elevated but manageable" and markets described as every other Friday. The Fed, OCC, and FDIC announced the Commercial Real Estate Stabilization Facility — a Special Purpose Vehicle allowing banks to transfer distressed CRE loans at carrying value rather than market value, with a government loss backstop. The SPV would "work through" the assets over ten years, managed by the same asset managers who had originated the problem. Treasury projected no taxpayer cost, assuming interest rates would decline, remote work would reverse, and office demand would return to 2019 levels. These assumptions were described as conservative.

*What could have happened:* The identical SPV mechanism directed toward conversion of distressed office and retail inventory into residential housing. Hundreds of millions of square feet of functionally obsolete office space in urban cores, in markets with severe residential supply shortfalls, at land values making conversion viable at a fraction of new construction cost — provided someone absorbed the basis risk between loan balance and converted asset value. That someone was going to be the government regardless. The choice was whether the government's loss absorption produced empty buildings managed by bank SPVs or housing units occupied by households priced out of those same cities by the preceding decade of QE-inflated asset prices. The government chose the former. Officials noted that housing policy was outside the mandate of a financial stability facility. The households who needed housing continued to need housing.

---

### Private Equity: The Carried Interest Preservation Act

#### *(Unofficial Title)*

The $2.4 trillion private credit market began experiencing what the industry called "idiosyncratic credit events" and a Delaware bankruptcy court called "a wave of overleveraged companies whose interest expense exceeded operating cash flow by a median of 40%." When defaults cascaded and model-based valuations collapsed, twenty-three state pension systems — covering teachers, firefighters, and municipal workers — faced insolvency. The PE sponsors whose portfolio companies were defaulting were not politically sympathetic, but the pension beneficiaries were, and their unions communicated this to every relevant legislator with considerable clarity. The resulting pension stabilization facility recapitalized the pension systems; the recapitalization flowed through existing allocations, restoring private credit fund values, preserving the management fee base on which carried interest was calculated. The teachers got their pensions. The sponsors kept their carry. The executives who had extracted dividend recapitalizations before the rate cycle hit retained those distributions. Several launched new funds.

*What could have happened:* The pension shortfall was a downstream consequence of the same interest rate policy deployed to reflate bank balance sheets after 2008 — a 7.5% assumed return with 10-year Treasury yields at 1.5% mathematically required reaching for yield in illiquid alternatives. The financial system bailed out in 2008 created the conditions requiring a pension bailout in 2027. A reparative framing would have clawed back some fraction of the returns extracted by PE sponsors during the cheap money era — through carried interest taxation, retroactive clawback legislation, or simply allowing funds to absorb the losses their limited partnership agreements specified they had accepted. None of this happened. The clawback provisions in limited partnership agreements are famously difficult to enforce, a feature that was not accidental.

### Government Debt: The Sovereign Restructuring That Will Not Be Called a Sovereign Restructuring

By 2028, US federal interest expense had reached $1.4 trillion annually, exceeding defense spending. The crisis didn't look like Argentina — it looked like Japan with worse demographics. The Fed implemented Yield Curve Control, capping 10-year Treasury yields while allowing inflation to run above that rate, meaning anyone holding Treasuries experienced a slow, polite, thoroughly deniable expropriation of their savings. The banks, as primary dealers required to hold Treasury inventory, were protected through a regulatory exemption of Treasury holdings from the Supplementary Leverage Ratio. Their cost of funding fell, their inventory was marked at par, their net interest margins expanded. Banking sector profitability during the Second Suppression was the highest in recorded history. This was not described as a bailout. It was monetary policy.

*What could have happened:* Negative real rates on government debt are a tax on savers — the question is always who is taxed and what the proceeds fund. The Second Suppression channeled the returns extracted from bond investors into sustaining the institutional architecture that had created the debt. An alternative framework would have used the same negative real rate environment to retire household debt — cheap mortgage refinancing at suppressed rates, student debt restructuring indexed to the inflation eroding its real value, consumer debt relief administered through the same banks receiving the SLR exemption. The monetary outcomes would have been equivalent. The distributional outcomes would have been unrecognizable. The Fed's mandate does not include distributional equity. This is simultaneously a fact and a choice — a fact about what the mandate says, and a choice about what it was written to say, by people who made decisions about whose interests monetary architecture should serve.

## CODA

The consistent thread across all six interventions — three retrospective, three prospective — is not that the mechanics were wrong. The SPV structure is sensible. Pension stabilization is legitimate policy. Yield Curve Control is a coherent monetary tool. Paying AIG's counterparties prevented a cascade that would have been genuinely catastrophic. The defenses are real.

What goes perpetually unasked is the prior question: *for whom is this sensible, legitimate, coherent?* In each case, the intervention that was technically available to benefit households at equivalent or lower systemic cost was not chosen. In each case, the intervention chosen produced its most proximate and largest benefit for the institutions and asset holders who had accumulated the risk during the preceding boom. In each case, the household benefit was described in the future tense — the recovery will generate jobs, the stable pensions will support consumption, the contained crisis will protect savings that many households do not hold.

The households were not in the room when the terms were set. They were told about it afterward, in a press release, which described the action as having been taken on their behalf.

This was partially true.

It always is.

That is the whole mechanism.
